# Competitive landscape scan

> Raw research output from the 2026-07-04 concept workflow (14 agents). Preserved verbatim for implementation reference; not edited for style.

TOOLS:
- WhatPulse (Free tier; Premium (personal) $29.99/year; Professional (business) $4/computer/month): Keystrokes, mouse clicks and mouse travel distance, network bandwidth (up/down, per app, per protocol, per country), computer uptime and 'Focused Uptime' (active vs idle), cross-platform (Windows/macOS/Linux), with a public leaderboard/community stats layer. Premium is $29.99/year (unlimited computers, per-app network/keystroke breakdowns, CSV export); a separate 'Professional' business tier bills $4/active computer/month for license and software-asset auditing. | Closest existing analog to ByteLife's input and network metric families, and literally uses the word 'Byte' as a username on its own leaderboard (coincidental, not a branded feature) -- but it has no disk I/O tracking, no AI/token awareness, and its framing is gamified community leaderboards rather than a personal productivity/digital-life dashboard, and it is Electron/cross-platform rather than a native menubar-first Mac experience.
- ActivityWatch (Free and open source): Automatic, fully local, open-source (MPL-2.0) time tracking: active window/app tracking and AFK (keyboard/mouse activity) detection out of the box, with optional watchers for browsers (Chrome/Firefox extensions) and editors (VS Code, Vim, Emacs). Cross-platform (Windows/macOS/Linux/Android), extensible via a plugin/watcher architecture and local REST API. | The strongest reference point for ByteLife's 'aggregate raw signals locally, no cloud' architecture and its extensible-watcher model, but it stops at time-in-app; it has no bytes-based framing (no network/disk I/O, no keystroke/mouse distance, no AI tokens), and its macOS builds lag behind and vary by release.
- RescueTime (Free tier; Premium $12/month billed annually (~$6.50/mo effective) or higher month-to-month): Automatic background time tracking of apps/websites, productivity scoring, Focus Sessions that block distracting apps/sites and silence notifications, goals/alerts, and a built-in task timer. Covers macOS, Windows, iOS, and browser extensions (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Safari, Arc, Brave). | Represents the mainstream cloud-synced 'productivity score' category ByteLife should differentiate from: RescueTime turns activity into a subjective judgment (productive/unproductive) rather than a neutral byte count, and it has no network, disk, input-distance, or AI-token dimension at all.
- Rize (From $9.99/month (annual) or $12.99/month; Professional $14.99/month adds client billing; 7-day trial): AI-assisted automatic time tracking on macOS/Windows: focus score, deep-work session detection, distraction frequency, AI-suggested breaks, app/site blocking, and built-in focus music. Explicitly does not track keystrokes, mouse movement, or screenshots -- only app/window/URL metadata. | Shows the premium end of the subscription-productivity-coach category and validates 'focus/attention' framing, but its explicit privacy stance of not tracking input activity is the inverse of ByteLife's premise, which treats keystrokes and mouse travel as first-class metrics rather than something to avoid.
- iStat Menus ($11.99-$18 one-time single license (varies by store), ~$29 family pack; also bundled in Setapp (~$9.99/month for the whole suite)): Native macOS menubar system monitor: CPU, GPU, memory, network (upload/download speed and bandwidth), disk usage and disk activity with S.M.A.R.T. status, temperatures, fans, and battery -- all live, low-overhead (~1% CPU, ~80-100MB RAM) menubar widgets. | The closest existing native-macOS UX and pricing template for ByteLife (menubar widgets, one-time purchase, low resource footprint) and the best proof that users already pay for granular network/disk byte counters, but it is purely a real-time system monitor with no historical trend dashboard, no input/keystroke tracking, no screen-time framing, and no AI awareness.
- Stats (exelban) (Free and open source): Free, fully open-source native macOS menubar system monitor (macOS 12+): CPU, GPU, memory, storage capacity and per-disk read/write speed and I/O activity, network upload/download speed and per-interface traffic totals, sensors/fans, battery, and a menubar clock. | Proves there is durable community demand for a free, open, native-Swift menubar stats app, and its per-disk read/write and network module are close structural cousins of two of ByteLife's five metric families, but it has zero historical/dashboard view (it's a live-only widget), no input tracking, no screen-time concept, and no AI-token awareness.
- Timing (Professional $108/yr, Expert $132/yr, Connect $192/yr): Automatic Mac time tracking down to the document/URL level, calendar/call/reminder integration, client-ready reports, and (in the top 'Connect' tier) AI integration via MCP plus team features and API/AppleScript export. Data stored locally by default; cloud sync is opt-in. | Demonstrates that Mac users will pay upwards of $100-190/year for a power-user time-tracking tool with API/automation hooks, and its new AI/MCP integration is an early, narrow signal that even legacy time trackers are starting to reach toward the AI-usage space ByteLife wants to own outright.
- Apple Screen Time (Free, built into macOS): Built into macOS: per-app and per-category usage time, pickup/notification counts (more complete on iOS), app/website time limits, downtime scheduling, and content/privacy restrictions, with a strong emphasis on parental controls and cross-device (Family Sharing) reporting. | Sets the free baseline and default user mental model for 'screen time,' but it is widely reported as inconsistent across devices/updates, offers no network, disk, input, or AI-token metrics, has a shallow and non-exportable history view, and is oriented around restriction/parental-control rather than a personal analytics dashboard -- leaving the term 'screen time' itself under-owned by any well-loved power-user tool.
- ccusage (Free and open source (npx ccusage)): Free open-source CLI (Node/npx) that parses local Claude Code (and now Codex, OpenCode, Amp, Gemini CLI, Copilot CLI, and other coding-agent) JSONL session logs into daily/weekly/monthly/session token and cost reports, with cache-token accounting and offline pricing data. | The de facto standard reference implementation for parsing Claude Code's local usage logs -- ByteLife's AI-usage metric family will likely need to solve the same parsing problem natively in Swift -- but ccusage is a terminal-only, pull-when-invoked tool with no persistent background daemon, no menubar presence, and no non-AI metrics.
- Tokens 4 Breakfast (Free tier (1 provider); Pro $7.99 one-time (all 8 providers, forecasting, alerts)): Native macOS menubar app tracking AI token usage and spend across 8 providers (Claude Code, Claude Web, OpenAI, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, OpenRouter, DeepSeek, Mistral): live rate-limit forecasting (e.g., predicting when you'll hit Claude's 5-hour/weekly caps), per-session budget alerts, subscription tracking, and a local-only SQLite store with no account or telemetry. | The single closest existing product to ByteLife's AI-usage metric family in both platform (native macOS menubar) and philosophy (local-first, one-time price, no cloud account) -- ByteLife's differentiation cannot be 'AI usage in the menubar,' since this already exists well-executed; it must be the fusion of AI tokens with the other four byte families that this app and its many GitHub-only cousins (Tokcat, ClaudeBar, TokenEater, Claude Monitor, Tokenscope, AI Token Monitor) do not attempt.

GAPS:
- No tool unifies AI token consumption with system-level byte metrics (network, disk, input, screen time) under one conceptual frame. The AI-usage trackers (Tokens 4 Breakfast, ccusage, Tokcat, ClaudeBar, TokenEater, Claude Monitor, TokenBar, Tokdash) are all AI-only aggregators; the system monitors (Stats, iStat Menus, WhatPulse) are all AI-blind. ByteLife's central premise -- that tokens, packets, disk blocks, screen-on time, and keystrokes are all facets of the same 'digital metabolism' -- has no existing product making that argument.
- No tool treats bytes as the universal unit across categories. Every existing app uses a category-specific unit (minutes for time trackers, dollars/tokens for AI trackers, GB for network tools) rather than reducing everything to a single comparable currency the way ByteLife's premise implies.
- Historical, exportable trend data is thin outside of the paid time-trackers. The free/open-source system monitors (Stats, ActivityWatch's watchers) are live-snapshot-oriented; only paid tools like Timing, RescueTime, and WhatPulse Premium offer meaningful history, and none of those paid tools include AI tokens or disk I/O.
- No tool covers local LLM usage (Ollama, LM Studio, MLX) alongside cloud AI usage in one personal dashboard. Local-LLM monitoring solutions found (SigNoz, MLflow, Anubis) are developer-observability tools, not personal digital-life trackers, and none of the consumer AI-usage menubar apps mention local models.
- Disk read/write byte tracking is oddly absent from the 'byte-lifestyle' trackers. WhatPulse tracks keystrokes/mouse/network/uptime but not disk I/O; Stats and iStat Menus show live disk activity but keep no history and have no keystroke/mouse/AI awareness. Nobody currently logs cumulative disk bytes read/written as a personal metric over time.
- No native, non-Electron, macOS-14+-only, Swift/SwiftUI menubar app currently spans all five of ByteLife's metric families; the field splits cleanly into single-family specialists.
- Mouse travel distance (cumulative physical distance, e.g., in meters/miles) is tracked only by WhatPulse among the surveyed tools; it is not present in any Apple-native or Stats/iStat-style tool, leaving room for a fresher take on that specific metric.

ABANDONMENT:
- Perceived data inaccuracy or uselessness: users lose trust and quit once they suspect the numbers are wrong or the categorization (e.g., 'productive' vs 'distracting') does not match their own judgment.
- Loss of tracking motivation over time: the initial novelty of seeing your own stats fades, and without a fresh hook (goals, social comparison, changing insights) engagement drops off, which research links to permanent abandonment more than any single technical flaw.
- Information overload: dashboards that surface too many numbers or too much granular detail at once cause confusion and frustration, pushing users toward disengagement rather than deeper insight.
- Boredom and declining novelty, similar to fitness-tracker and mobile-health-app attrition patterns, where users stop opening the app once checking it stops feeling rewarding.
- Friction from the tracking mechanism itself: for time/screen trackers specifically, users cite battery drain, permission prompts (Accessibility/Screen Recording/Full Disk Access on macOS), inconsistent cross-device sync, or restart-required background helpers as reasons they disable the tool.
- Subscription fatigue for a category users perceive as 'a simple utility': multiple sources note real user pushback against recurring fees for menubar-scale tools, favoring one-time purchases instead, which suggests subscription pricing itself is a distinct abandonment driver in this niche.
- Guilt or anxiety from the data (a softer, less-cited but recurring theme in the quantified-self literature): when tracked numbers become a source of self-judgment rather than curiosity, some users disengage to avoid the discomfort.

DIFFERENTIATION:
- Own the single-currency framing: report every metric family (AI tokens, network, disk, screen time, input) normalized into or alongside a literal byte count, which no competitor does; this is ByteLife's most defensible conceptual moat given the survey found zero unified byte-framed dashboards.
- Be the first native Swift/SwiftUI menubar app to fuse AI-token tracking with classic system-byte tracking; the AI-usage niche (Tokens 4 Breakfast and its many GitHub cousins) and the system-monitor niche (Stats, iStat Menus) are currently populated by disjoint sets of small apps that do not cross over.
- Match the pricing norm this niche has already validated: one-time purchase (the iStat Menus/Tokens 4 Breakfast/Bartender pattern, roughly $8-20) rather than a subscription, since subscription fatigue is an explicit, named abandonment driver for simple utilities and several direct comparables (iStat Menus $11.99-18, Tokens 4 Breakfast $7.99, Bartender $20) already prove the model works at this price point.
- Add local LLM usage (Ollama/LM Studio/MLX) to the AI metric family from day one, since every AI-usage competitor found only covers cloud providers (Claude, OpenAI, Cursor, Copilot, etc.), leaving local inference completely uncovered by any personal-tracking tool.
- Track disk read/write bytes cumulatively over time, not just live speed, which no surveyed tool does (Stats and iStat Menus show live-only disk activity with no history; WhatPulse skips disk entirely).
- Design explicitly against the two dominant abandonment causes found in the literature: build in a slowly-updating, always-fresh 'digital life' narrative (streaks, deltas, milestones on cumulative byte counts) to counter motivation loss, and keep the default view to one clear number-of-the-day rather than a dense multi-panel dashboard to counter information overload.
- Lean into neutral, non-judgmental framing (raw byte counts, not a 'productivity score') as a point of contrast with RescueTime and Rize, whose value proposition is explicitly evaluative; this also sidesteps the tracking-anxiety abandonment driver noted in the quantified-self research.
- Support data export/API/local storage as table stakes (following ActivityWatch, Timing, and Tokens 4 Breakfast's local-first precedent) to earn trust with the privacy-conscious power-user audience this category skews toward.
- Consider a lightweight social or leaderboard layer only as an optional add-on, borrowing WhatPulse's proven community-stats hook for motivation, but keep it off by default given the privacy expectations set by every other modern competitor in this survey.
